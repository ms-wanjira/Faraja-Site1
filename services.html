Alchohol Addiction

Do you worry that your drinking is becoming a little bit too much?

Are you losing control of your drinking habits?

Alcohol use and dependency is on the rise in Kenya due to unhealthy drinking habits, easy access to alcohol and lack of information on the dangers of excessive drinking.

Faraja Fountain offers effective Evidence Based and affordable counselling services for people with alcohol dependency. We understand that clients are unique hence, offer individualized treatment to suit our client's situations and needs. We offer both traditional one on one counseling and e-therapy sessions.

Through screening and assessment, our qualified addiction will advise you on the kind of treatment which suits you or your loved one best. Some clients may require a more intensive level of care, while others may have other co-occurring disorders, and in this case, we will be able to refer appropriately.

How do you know that you need help?

Alcohol use passes several stages before it becomes problematic. Alcohol dependence is characterized by a strong, often uncontrollable desire to use. The more accustomed the body becomes to the effects of alcohol, the more the tolerance grows and the more you have to increase the use more than before to experience the same 'high' as before. Tolerance often results in physical dependence on the substance, which can develop into alcohol addiction.

When you become dependent on alcohol, even though you may not realize that your drinking is out of control, drinking becomes your priority and the more you seek opportunities to drink more than anything else, including family, friendships and work. This is usually unintentional but can be misconstrued as lack of care for loved ones and alcohol takes over your life.

Alcohol can be a coping mechanism for other deeper issues because it provides a “relief” from the underlying issues but unfortunately it only makes things worse and the problems can quickly spiral to uncontrollable levels.

Drug Addiction and Prevention

Do you Know the signs of drug addiction?

Where does the line between use and abuse lie?

What qualifies as drug addiction?

Drug addiction is a severe problem affecting thousands of people across all age groups social backgrounds in Kenya. Addiction is a chronic incurable disease, but it can be managed.

Addiction carries a lot of stigma and stereotyping due to lack of understanding and plenty of people think that alcohol and drug use is a matter of choice and will power. Contrary to that common belief that addiction is a chronic condition and if not properly managed it can get worse with time and the person loses their power of choice and can no longer control their consumption or use.

The number of people with active substance use disorder is very high in Kenya and includes people using a variety of substances. Some of the commonly abused substances in kenya includes but not limited to:

  • Marijuana
  • Prescription Drugs
  • Khat(Miraa)
  • Heroine
  • Cocaine
  • Benzodiazepines
  • Methamphetamines

    • Crossing the line from drug abuse to addiction

    There are different stages of drug use ranging from normal to problematic use. Not everyone who uses alcohol and/or drugs develops a substance use disorder. Some people can stop use/consumption whenever they want while others cannot control their use.

    Signs of Drugs of Addiction.

    1. Drinking or using greater amounts of alcohol or drugs, or drinking or using for a longer period than originally intended.
    2. Trying to cut back on or control alcohol or drug use, or trying to quit entirely, but not being able to.
    3. Spending a lot of time drinking or using drugs, or being sick as a direct result of drinking or drug use.
    4. Noticing signs of craving, meaning wanting a drink or a drug so badly that it crowds out all other thoughts.
    5. Drinking or drug use, or being sick as a result, interferes with responsibilities at home, work, or school.
    6. Continuing to drink or use drugs despite the problems it causes with friends or family.
    7. Cutting back on or giving up activities that were once important if they get in the way of drinking or drug use.
    8. Ending up in risky, dangerous, or life-threatening situations as a result of drinking or drug use (i.e. walking in a dangerous area, unsafe sex, operating machinery, driving under the influence).
    9. Continuing to drink or use drugs despite their harmful impact on physical or mental health.
    10. Needing to drink or use greater amounts to achieve the desired effect (developing a tolerance).
    11. Experiencing withdrawal symptoms when the effects of drugs or alcohol wear off.

    Someone who shows even 2 of these symptoms may qualify as having an alcohol or substance use disorder. The severity of a person's condition is determined by the number of qualifications they meet:

    • Mild Use Disorder: 2 to 3 symptoms.
    • Moderate Use Disorder: 4 to 5 symptoms.
    • Severe Use Disorder: 6 or more symptoms.

    The best way to determine the presence of a substance or alcohol use disorder is to seek professional help from a Qualified Addiction Professional.

    Will drug addiction treatment actually cure drug addiction?

    Will the desire to drink or use drugs ever go away?

    Unfortunately, there isn't a cure for alcoholism or drug addiction. Addiction is a chronic, relapsing brain disorder similar to other conditions. There's always the possibility of relapse, and at Faraja Fountain we help you on how to manage your alcohol or substance use disorder so that you can live a productive healthy lifestyle free from drugs.

    Recovery Coaching & Relapse Prevention.

    Recovery from drug and alcohol addiction is a lifelong process, which requires a commitment to a plan of recovery. The leading causes of relapse are failure to have a plan of recovery or deviation from that plan.

    Our relapse prevention program is designed to provide the client with an in-depth understanding of the triggers, daily stressors, or anxieties which can be troubling for someone in recovery. Relapse behaviors emerge long before the person takes the first use of the substance, it is essential that someone in recovery attends a relapse prevention program.

    Recovery coaching helps in establishing healthy boundaries, assisting with development of refusal skills, conflict resolution, effective decision making techniques, healthy choice and dealing with urges or craving.
